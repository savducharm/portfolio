Dr. Vocab

Rapid Prototyping and User Testing

The Challenge

The goal of this project was to create an intuitive and easy-to-use vocabulary learning mobile app to help higher education students achieve better retention and understanding of essential vocabulary for their field of study.

Target Base

  • 18 to 30 years old
  • English speakers
  • Higher-education students

Tools Used

  • Adobe XD
  • Pencil and Paper
  • Keynote
  • Marvel

The Process

For my approach, I followed Design Thinking and Iterative design processes. However, given the short time span of the project (3 weeks), not all stages were executed fully.

Step 1

Research

The first step of this project was to conduct research on the competition and potential users. The goal was to identify opportunities to stand out in the market and second, to understand my users’ thoughts, feelings, and past experiences concerning learning vocabulary.

Competitor Analysis

I conducted a competitive analysis of other vocabulary learning apps on the market.

In looking at Quizlet, Flash Cards Flashcard Maker, and Magoosh, I gained insight into what users liked, disliked, and expected from vocabulary learning apps.

Weaknesses
  • Too many options for customization can lead to overwhelm and decision fatigue during studying (an already stressful time). Too few options for customization leads to frustration.
  • Apps without positive reinforcement (i.e. unlocking new levels), progress tracking, or gamification were not regarded as highly.
  • Advertisements can be extremely distracting and pose significant challenges to learning and retaining vocabulary.
Our Soultions
  • Provide the right amount of customization to not overwhelm the user.
  • Create a positive and engaging experience through the gamification of learning new words.
  • Design a simple, clean interface with minimal distraction. Keep advertising to a minimum or using non-invasive ways to advertise.

User Research

With the insights I gained from the competitor analysis, the next step was to understand the behaviors, thoughts, and feelings that potential users experience with educational or vocabulary learning apps.

User Interviews

I conducted in-person and phone interviews with four people, all within the age range of 25-35, who I recruited from my personal network of friends and family.

In conducting these interviews with four people from a similar demographic but with different behaviors and perspectives, I gained fascinating insight.

The interview script can be reviewed here.

Main Insights
  • Participants expressed the importance of context when learning new thing, i.e., understanding how, where, and when a word or concept is used.
  • Participants felt that Learning roots can potentially help you remember words better and feel more confident guessing and learning new words.
  • Participants liked having friends of a community to learn with as it is more motivating and helpful.
  • I found that personal interest and having an inner desire to be better were key motivators for my participates in using study aids.
Feature Insights
  • Allowing users to enter sentences that use a vocabulary word in context would be helpful.
  • Allowing users to add roots to vocabulary words could empower users to retain and grasp words more easily.
  • Having some sort of social network or sharing within the app could serve to motivate users.
  • The app should be a tool for users to better themselves and their learning and thus aimed at those in higher education or those who already have a desire to learn.
Step 2

Establishing my User Base

After the research stage, I moved on to establish my user base by creating a persona, user stories, job stories, and user flows. These helped me to define the problems the app would be aiming to solve as well as how, for who, and in what context.

Persona

Using my interviewees as inspiration, my persona took the form of Elizabeth, a 28 year old medical student at UCLA.

With such a busy schedule but a drive to better her medical knowledge, Elizabeth needs a way to study and record the new vocabulary and medical jargon she is learning and hearing everyday. She needs something easy-to-use and straightforward, something that holds her interest, keeps track of her progress, and allows her to study in short bursts on her schedule.

Elizabeth represents a student in higher education who actively wants to expand her vocabuluary to ensure she grows her knowledge, not only for school but her career afer.

Elizabeth is a highly motivated student who was to maximize her efficency in studying and learning new medical jargon and key vocabulary.

User Stories and Job Stories

To empathize with Elizabeth further, I wrote out user stories and job stories to capture her needs and desires in using a vocabulary app.

These helped me to identify potential useful features that would allow Elizabeth to meet her personal goals.

Problem and Hypothesis Statement

With a better understanding of who my user is and what her goals in using the app would be, I wrote a problem and hypothesis statement. These served as my north star during the ideation and design stage, as they reminded me for whom and what I was designing.

Problem Statement

Elizabeth (a busy student) needs a tool to learn, study, and record medical vocabulary in an easy and non-time-consuming way because she wants to master and better understand medical terminology for school and her career in medicine.

Hypothesis Statement

We believe that by creating an intuitive and easy-to-use app that presents words in context and provides definitions of word roots, along with the ability to customize and add words, hear pronunciations, and connect with friends and other users, for Elizabeth, we will enable her to achieve better retention and understanding of medical terms for use during her studies and later on in her career in the medical field.

Step 3

Information Architecture

Once I had a more solidified idea of my users and their goals with the creation of Elizabeth's persona, I was then able to begin constructing the information architecture of the app with task analyses and user flows.

User Flows

I began to create user flows of critical actions my users would want to complete based on Elizabeth. These allowed me to get a better idea of what my users would need to accomplish their goals.

What are the most important tasks for Elizabeth to be able to complete?

I identified the two most important tasks for a user:

  • Task 1: User Goal: Add new flashcard
  • Task 2: User Goal: Quiz on word roots
Step 4

Wireframes and Rapid Prototyping

Having started the information architecture, the next step was to begin designing the actual screens. Given the limited time for this project, I performed rapid prototyping and created all wireframes with paper and pencil and then turned them into a clickable prototype using Marvel.

Rapid Ideation

Round 1

I used the “Crazy 8 style” to begin brainstorming possible solutions. Within an eight-minute time span I designed 8 possible screen variations for each screen needed.

Round 2

I then went back and looked over all the frames. I then took the frames I liked the most and recreated them so they were more clean and neat.

Rapid Low-Fidelity Prototype

With the revised wireframes, I was able to then create the first prototype. I did this by taking photos of the wireframes and uploading them to Marvel.

I made sure to consult my task analyses and user flows to ensure I had all the needed screens for users to complete specific tasks.

Step 5

Testing with Real Users

Having created a clickable prototype of the app, the next step was putting the designs to the test with real users.

User Testing

The goal of this usability test was to assess the navigation and key features of the prototype for users using Dr. Vocab for the first time. I wanted to see if users would be able to intuitively move through the app and what, if any, pain points they might experience.

I tested the prototype with 5 participants in-person on an iPhone 7, asking to conduct 5 different scenario tasks within a 10-minute session.

The usability test script can be reviewed here.

The key tasks were to:
  • Create a new account
  • Log in and log out
  • Add a new card to an existing deck
  • Complete quiz
  • Search for a new deak and add it personal library

Results

Based on my observations during the usability tests, I rated users’ actions or inactions using Jakob Nielson’s error severity rating scale (0-4).

Overall, the participants did not experience many pain points. Several expressed positive feedback and found the tasks easy to complete. However, some usability issues did occur that needed to be addressed.

Key Usability Issues
  • On-boarding screens were confusing (medium severity)
  • Some users were unsure of where they were on the navigation bar (medium severity)
  • When adding a new word, users didn’t realize that they could flip that card to add a definition (high severity)
  • Several users wanted to be able to review their scores after the quiz (low severity)
Actionable Steps
  • Change onboarding copy to be more detailed and readable.
  • Add some arrows (or similar) to let users know to continue to other screens.
  • Make tab change color or style to let user know where they are at any given time.
  • Make the arrow on the card larger and more prominent to allow users to know they can flip a card.
  • Put “Save” button on separate, second page so user must flip card to save the new card.
  • Add in function to review answers.
Step 6

Revised Prototype

Using the results of the usability test, I revisited the wireframes and made changes to fix the key usability issues I observed. I created a mid-fidelity prototype in Adobe XD with these new changes.

The main changes I made were:

  1. For the on-boarding screens, I added arrows so users knew that they could continue forward.
  2. I updated the navigation bar to change colors when on a certain tab.
  3. For the adding a new card feature, I included an obvious arrow to trigger users to flip the card and push the “save” button on the back side of the card.
  4. I added a “review answer” function to enable users to see their mistakes during a quiz.

With these changes and an overall cleaner design, I created a revised prototype that can be found in its entirety here.

User Onboarding

Observation

Onboarding screens confusing for every participant. What to do was not intuitive leaving participants stuck.

Severity: 3

Recommendation
  • Make on-boarding screens more detailed with text that can be read.
  • Add some arrows or some other indicator to let users know to continue.
Solution

Added arrows on the on-boarding pages to let users know how to continue forward.

Navigation Bar

Observation

User unsure what part of the navigation tab they were on.

Severity: 2

Recommendation
  • Make tab change color or style to let user know where they are at a given time.
Solution

When on a given navigation tab, added color to tab to let users know where they are.

Adding New Flashcard

Observation

Users unaware of how to add definitions when creating a new flashcard.

Severity: 4

Recommendation
  • Add more prominent arrow to card.
  • Put “Save” button on other page so user has to flip card to save the new card.
Solution

Added more clear arrows to the add new card page and moved “save” button to second page so users know they can add more information.

Vocabulary Quiz

Observation

Several users wanted to be able to review quiz answers after a quiz.

Severity: 2

Recommendation
  • Add function that enables users to review quiz answers.
Solution

Added in “Review Answers” function for users.

Looking Ahead

Further Improvements

I would recommend further developing the app into a high-fidelity prototype that expands more of the key features our users would want to see. Then, test the prototype to determine if the more advanced features are easy to use and understand.

Going forward, there remains much to be done to fully develop this app.

The main improvements I would recommend are:

  1. There are key functions that Elizabeth, our persona, wants in a vocabulary app, such as pronunciations and a community function where she can connect with other users and friends.
  2. As for striking a good balance between too much choice and not enough, I think we did a good job. There is still room to add more customization options and abilities.
  3. Gamification is lacking in the current prototype. In future iterations, this is the most important item to improve upon. I’d like to see more engaging quizzing options, along with tracking capabilities and ways to challenge other users.

Conclusions

Overall, going through this process taught me a lot about what goes into designing an app, even a limited prototype.

Conducting user interviews and creating a key persona really helped in the design process to keep me on track, especially because it can be quite overwhelming to know where to begin, what to include in the design, and how to approach all the other design decisions I had to make. Having real users to think about helped me make better and more informed decisions.

Something else I learned, and enjoyed a lot, was interacting with users. It was fascinating to see how differently people think and behave. Users will surprise you! They all have such different perspectives and ideas.

And lastly, trying to make things perfect isn’t the point. Flaws are to be expected. It is more productive to create an incomplete project that can be iterated upon and learn from the process and your user than to never complete a project.
