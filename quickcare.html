QuickCare

UX/UI Case Study

The Challenge

The goal of this project was to empower a broad demographic of users to easily find the board-certified doctors and specialists they need.

I sought to create a user-friendly mobile telehealth application and accompanying desktop site that would connect users with doctors and allow them to schedule virtual appointments and seek immediate medical advice without the hassle or tedium of dealing with insurance, referrals, and multiple in-office visits.

Target Base

  • 18 to 65 years old
  • Living in the U.S.

Tools Used

  • Adobe XD
  • Adobe Illustrator
  • Pencil and Paper
  • OptimalSort
  • Keynote
  • Google Forms

The Process

For my approach, I followed Design Thinking and Iterative design processes. See image below for details.

Step 1

Research

To begin this project, the first crucial stage was to conduct research to establish who my user base is and what they want and need from a telehealth app. I also needed to evaluate the competition and market overall.

Competitor Analysis

I started with a competitor analysis to get a better idea of the current market for telehealth apps. I looked at Teladoc, a leading telehealth app in both the Apple App store and Google Play store, and K Health, an up-and-coming telehealth app that offers AI medical care that can be followed up on by a doctor during a video call. I created SWOT profiles based on in-depth looks at each competitor's key objectives, overall strategy, market advantage, marketing profile, and UX design.

Weaknesses
  • Despite the ease of accessing doctors from your phone, both apps use internal vetting systems that prohibit users from being able to select and talk directly with the doctor of their choice.
  • Users of these apps often experience long wait times, feel rushed during their visits, and report that the doctors are impatient.
  • Apps like K Health come off as “cold,” especially to older demographics, because they lack human-to-human interaction.
Our Soultions
  • Empower users to access specialists without having to acquire a referral from a primary care physician or go to multiple doctors in order to see the specialist they wanted to see from the very beginning.
  • Increase access to doctors by allowing users to schedule appointments with the doctors of their choosing, rather than only offering immediate care appointments with on-call doctors.
  • Encourage human-to-human interaction via phone calls, video calls, and direct messaging.

User Research

The next step was to get a better idea of people’s experiences with healthcare and telehealth in the U.S. To do this, I conducted an online survey and in-person interviews.

User Surveys

Goals:

  1. Better understand people’s good points and pain points within the U.S. healthcare system.
  2. Gauge people’s openness to using telehealth over traditional in-person doctor visits.
  3. Discover which apps, websites, or other services people use when it comes to healthcare: Do people use Google to search for information about odd symptoms? How do they schedule their doctor appointments? Do they talk with a 24-hour nurse? Do they use an app to access health documents?

I shared the survey with my own personal network and kept it live for 1 week. During the time it was live, I received a total of 42 responses.

The survey can be found here.

Though the survey provided a lot of information, the key takeaways were:

  • Most people reported going to the doctor only 1-3 times a year.
  • The main reason people reported going to the doctor was for flu symptoms, pharmaceuticals prescriptions/refills, injury, and ‘other.’
  • Most people felt their co-pay fee was moderately expensive to very expensive and 42.5% stated their fees very often or somewhat often prevented them from seeking care.
  • Over half reported that they were very likely to use a search engine to research illness symptoms.
  • About 80% stated they would be somewhat likely or very likely to use a telehealth app.

User Interviews

With the insights I gained from the survey, I put together a set of questions for in-person interviews to learn more about people’s health perceptions and openness to telehealth apps. I interviewed three people, intentionally choosing people from different ages, genders, and stages in life, for approximately 30 mins each.

The interview script can be reviewed here.

From these three interviews, the key takeaways were:

  • None had tried a telehealth app, but all were open to trying one.
  • The process of seeking healthcare is often frustrating and long. From finding a good local doctor, seeking out a specialist, filling out forms, calling to ask a question, etc., the process is not enjoyable.
  • The main goal when seeking healthcare is to get a quick diagnostic and immediate, actionable advice to resolve the issue.
  • Having a rating system for healthcare professionals and ability to see credentials would be a valuable feature in a telehealth app.
  • Being able to share as much information with a doctor via an app is essential. This would include giving doctor access to medical history, vitals, sharing photos, and more.

Affinity Mapping

To wrap up the research stage, I took the free responses from my user survey and pulled quotes from my user interviews to create an affinity map. The goal of creating an affinity map was to group together similar experiences, needs, wants, and pain points concerning healthcare and telehealth.

A more complete breakdown of this process can be found here.

Main Insights
  • Participants want a vetting system to determine if they need to see a doctor because seeing a doctor is costly, in terms of both time and money.
  • Participants want real care and solutions for effective and efficient treatment as soon as possible from competent professionals.
  • Many participants expressed frustration with the process for finding and seeing a specialist.
  • Participants want convenience and immediacy when seeking healthcare.
  • Participants expressed frustration with finding local, knowledgeable, and available doctors covered by their insurance plans and within their area.
Feature Insights
  • Participants want an easy-to-use and easily accessible application.
  • Participants want to ensure the doctors they speak to have access to enough information to provide an accurate diagnosis and treatment plan.
  • People are not willing to pay more than their normal co-pay to speak with a doctor via the app.
  • Participants want to be able to speak immediately to the right doctor for their needs.
  • Participants want access to medical professionals’ credentials and a rating system.
  • Overall, participants want to save time and have a more convenient way of getting medical care.
Step 2

Establishing my User Base

After the research stage, I had a much better understanding of my users’ wants, needs, pain points, and expectations. With these new insights, I began to create user stories to outline each possible goal my users might have.

Personas

After flushing out my user stories, I created personas to make my users come to life. Throughout the design process, I came back to these personas at every step to ensure I was making decisions that would meet their needs.

Lisa represents a younger demographic who is generally healthy and tech-savvy. However, she does not have the money to spend on multiple doctor visits for just one issue.

Joseph represents those in their 30s to 40s. As a traveling businessman he is always on the go and in the prime of his career. For this reason, he wants to spend as little time as possible finding the perfect care.

Stephanie represents an older demographic who is less tech-savvy. As a mom juggling her kids’ schedules, Lisa wants a one-stop-shop where she can take care of all their health needs without sacrificing quality.

User Journeys

To take a deeper dive into the desires and perspectives of my personas, I created user journey maps.

Lisa’s journey made clear the hesitation new users may experience when signing up for a new app and learning the process. When designing the user onboarding process, it was my goal to keep it simple and easy.

Joseph’s journey made me realize how convenient it would be to directly message your doctor without having to schedule an appointment. I was sure to include this in my design.

Stephanie’s journey caused me to take into account the importance of trust users will want to establish with the doctors and the app overall. I aimed to design the app with this in mind.

Step 3

Information Architecture

Once I had a more solidified idea of my users and their goals from my user stories, personas, and flows, I was then able to begin establishing a sitemap that accounted for the hierarchy and organization of pages.

Sitemap

Card Sort

To get a better idea of how users would intuitively navigate a telehealth app, I conducted virtual open card sorting with 5 participants who were given 30 cards to sort and name using OptimalSort.

The resulting matrix and dendrograms showed distinct grouping patterns and possible names for navigation.

Refined Sitemap

Using the results of the card sort, I refined my initial sitemap to ensure it was more intuitive for users.

Step 5

Testing with Real Users

Having created high-fidelity, clickable prototypes of the app and desktop versions, the next step was putting the designs to the test with real users.

User Testing

The goal of this usability test was to assess the learnability, errors, and satisfaction for new users using QuickCare for the first time. I wanted to see if new users would be able to navigate, understand, and enjoy using the app and its features, such as searching for a doctor, scheduling an appointment, and messaging a doctor.

The key objectives were to:

  • Determine how easily participants can navigate through the app.
  • Observe if completing tasks are intuitive for participants (i.e., searching for a doctor, scheduling an appointment, and messaging a doctor).
  • Assess the value of the app and to what extent participants enjoy using it.

I tested 6 participants recruited from my own personal network of friends and family. Participants ranged from early 20s to late 50s to ensure a wide age demographic to fit personas.

Each participant tested both the app and desktop versions. I alternated the versions participants tested first to make sure results were not influenced by previous knowledge.

Results

I analyzed the data using a Rainbow spreadsheet, task satisfaction ratings, task errors, and other observations.

Most of the participants found QuickCare easy to use and expressed positive feedback. However, there were critical issues that needed to be resolved before launch to ensure users would be able to execute fundamental actions within the app.

To see full analysis of the test, please click here.

Key Usability Issues
  • Arrow touch points/targets too small (high severity)
  • Difficulty ending video call (medium severity)
  • Participants unaware of scrolling on certain pages/windows (high severity)
  • Difficulty finding the “Waiting Room” (medium severity)
  • Updating the information on the onboarding pages (high severity)
Actionable Steps
  • Make arrow touch points/targets on mobile and desktop versions larger for easier clicking.
  • Remove the doctor’s name on the video call and replace it with an icon that pops up the “end call” and “mute mic” options.
  • Add a scroll bar on the side of the screen/window to indicate there is more content.
  • Change the name of “Waiting Room” to “Immediate Care.”
  • Update the information on the onboarding pages to ensure users know this is a telehealth app.
Step 6

Further Iterating and Visual Design

After testing the functionality of QuickCare and fixing all major usability issues, the next step was to further the visual design of the interface. During this stage, I conducted a user preference test and a peer review, as well as consulted iOS Human Interaction Guidelines and established accessibility guidelines.

Preference Testing and Peer Review

During the visual design process, I touched base with my users via preference testing to get a better idea of the style to which they were more inclined. Additionally, I reached out to other UX designers for feedback on my designs.

Accessibility Guidelines

Once my visual design began to take shape, I consulted the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) to ensure accessibility and readability. This step really changed my design and made it more cohesive.

Visual Design Process

Using grids, Gestalt principles, color theory, and the insights gained from the preference testing, peer review, WCAG, and iOS Human Interaction Guidelines, I iterated on my original grayscale wireframes to design the final, polished version.

Onboarding Pages

  1. Added grid and colors, as well as refined the onboarding illustrations and buttons.
  2. Refined color palette and complied with WCAG and iOS guidelines.

Home Page

  1. Added grid and colors. Made the design more cohesive, warm, and welcoming.
  2. Took out nav bar, as it proved to be redundant and unnecessary. Complied with WCAG and iOS guidelines.

Doctor Search

  1. Added grid and colors. Expanded amount of doctor information and credentials.
  2. Refined grid. Simplified overall look by removing unnecessary or confusing features.

Doctor Profile

  1. Added grid and colors. Added more white space between elements for a cleaner feel.
  2. Refined grid which allowed for more usage of the entire screen making a more balanced look.

Direct Messaging

  1. Added grid and colors.
  2. Complied with WCAG and iOS guidelines. Created more white space for a more refined look.

Final Design and Conclusions

Working on this project was intimidating due to the amount of competition on the market. However, as we as a society come to rely more and more on our technology, human-to-human contact is becoming a rarity. This became apparent once I delved deeper into the needs and concerns of my users. QuickCare sought to bridge the gap between technology and human intimacy by purposefully putting the user at the forefront of each design decision.

Moreover, creating this app during a pandemic made me see how important telehealth is and will continue to be. For that reason, human-centered design is crucial going forward in this industry.

Overall, working on this project was eye-opening and enjoyable.
